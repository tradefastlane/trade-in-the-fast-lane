# Trade in the Fast Lane: Crypto-Life Design

## Core idea

This is a modern crypto-life strategy game inspired by the time-pressure structure of
*Jones in the Fast Lane*. Players share a city and a changing economy, but they act
simultaneously instead of waiting for individual turns.

The match is divided into one-minute "weeks." Every player receives 60 action minutes
at the start of a week. Actions may be taken in any order until that allowance is
spent. Trading remains available between life actions and does not consume action
minutes.

## The player loop

1. Travel to a useful city location.
2. Spend time studying, working, eating, resting or shopping.
3. Use qualifications to obtain better careers and unlock special apartment actions.
4. Trade crypto as a risky secondary source of wealth.
5. Improve health, housing, possessions and happiness.
6. Finish with the best life-adjusted net worth.

## City locations

- **Apartment:** rest, manage housing and attempt computer-based side projects.
- **Chain Academy:** study qualifications.
- **Gig & Career Hub:** apply for jobs and clock in for paid work.
- **Satoshi Snacks:** buy food to restore hunger, health and energy.
- **Block & Bed:** buy furniture, computers and valuable possessions.
- **Fast Lane Exchange:** thematic home of the crypto trading desk.

Travel currently costs six action minutes.

## Qualifications

| Qualification | Study needed | Main unlocks |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Programming | 60 | Junior developer work; prerequisite for technical courses |
| Blockchain Engineering | 80 | Protocol careers and an underground-market prototype |
| Cybersecurity | 80 | Security analyst work and fictional bug-bounty attempts |
| Quantum Computing | 120 | Elite research work |
| Social Engineering | 60 | Risky persuasion hustles |
| Online Marketing | 50 | Community work and viral campaigns |

One study session costs 20 action minutes. The longest qualification therefore takes
six sessions, or two perfect one-minute weeks if the player spends every available
minute studying.

## Jobs

Jobs differ by qualification, pay, happiness and action-time cost. A player travels
to the Career Hub, applies for a qualifying job, and then clocks in for shifts. Entry
work is always available, while technical qualifications unlock progressively better
careers.

## Apartment actions

These are intentionally fictional, abstract risk-and-reward rolls rather than real
instructions:

- **Security bug bounty:** legal security challenge with a chance of a payout.
- **Persuasion hustle:** a risky fictional sales attempt that may earn money or a fine.
- **Viral token campaign:** audience-building gamble that affects money and reputation.
- **Underground market prototype:** a high-risk fictional venture with large possible
  rewards and consequences.

Future balancing can make furniture and computers modify success odds, time costs and
maximum rewards.

## Life systems

- Hunger decreases as weeks pass.
- Low hunger damages health and happiness.
- Food restores hunger and may restore health or energy.
- Rest consumes time but restores energy and some health.
- Better housing and possessions increase happiness.
- Expensive possessions may appreciate, incur insurance premiums, or be lost in random
  incidents when uninsured.

## Trading

Crypto trading is a sideline rather than the whole game. Players can:

- search and verify live crypto assets;
- open multiple long or short positions;
- use 1x to 100x leverage;
- set simplified stop-loss, take-profit and trailing-exit levels;
- close positions manually;
- see open trades beneath each player in the public standings.

Market quotes and shared game state target a ten-second update rhythm. The central BTC
chart is a public spectator element suitable for streaming or a television display.

## Characters

Players receive fictional crypto-culture personas such as the Laser-Eyed Maximalist,
Mysterious Protocol Founder or Anonymous Cypherpunk. These are parody archetypes rather
than real people, which keeps criminal or embarrassing random events safely fictional.

## Winning

The current formula remains:

`Final score = net worth × happiness multiplier`

This allows a healthier, happier player to defeat a slightly richer rival without
making wealth irrelevant. Health, reputation and career achievements can become small
tiebreakers after playtesting.
